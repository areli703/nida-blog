# How to Start a Web Design Business with SiteSwan in One Week

You don’t need a design degree, a coding bootcamp certificate, or a Silicon Valley address to start a web design business. You need a platform, a plan, and the willingness to talk to strangers.

This guide walks you from zero to first client in seven days using a white-label website builder. While the examples reference SiteSwan, the strategy applies to any reseller platform—including newer alternatives built for higher-margin agencies.

---

## Day 1: Sign Up and Pick Your Niche

**Morning:**
- Sign up for a reseller plan.
- Set up your custom domain (e.g., `youragency.com`) and point it to the platform.
- Upload your logo and brand colors.

**Afternoon:**
- Pick **one** niche. Examples:
  - HVAC / home services
  - Local dentists
  - Real estate agents
  - Restaurants in your city

**Why one niche?** Because a generic “I build websites” pitch is invisible. A “I build websites for HVAC companies in Phoenix” pitch is memorable.

**Action:** Build a 1-page “coming soon” landing page for your agency with a headline targeting your niche.

---

## Day 2: Build Your Demo Site

You need a portfolio piece before you have clients.

- Choose a template related to your niche.
- Swap in fake-but-realistic copy (use ChatGPT if needed).
- Add real-looking photos from Unsplash or Pexels.
- Publish it to a demo subdomain or your main domain.

**Pro tip:** Include a small “Built by [Your Agency]” link in the footer. This turns every demo into a lead generator.

**Time budget:** 3–4 hours. Don’t over-polish.

---

## Day 3: Set Your Pricing

Create three tiers. Most prospects self-select into the middle one.

| Tier | Name | Price | Includes |
|------|------|-------|----------|
| Basic | Starter Site | $99/mo or $799 one-time | 1–3 pages, mobile-friendly, contact form |
| Standard | Business Site | $199/mo or $1,499 one-time | 5–7 pages, SEO setup, blog, 1 month of edits |
| Premium | Growth Site | $399/mo or $2,500 one-time | 10+ pages, e-commerce, analytics, quarterly updates |

**Rule:** Never quote a price in a vacuum. Always anchor against the value (e.g., “One new customer from this site pays for a year of service”).

## Day 4: Outreach (Templates Included)

You need 50–100 touchpoints to land your first meeting. Use 3 channels: cold email, LinkedIn, and in-person networking (if local).

### Cold Email Template

**Subject:** Quick question about [Business Name]’s site

> Hi [First Name],
>
> I was looking for [Business Type] in [City] and came across [Business Name]. I noticed your site [isn’t mobile-friendly / looks outdated / doesn’t show up on Google].
>
> I help [niche] businesses get a modern site that actually brings in leads—usually within a week.
>
> Would you be open to a 10-minute call to see if it’s a fit? No pressure either way.
>
> Best,  
> [Your Name]  
> [Your Agency]  
> [Your Phone]

### LinkedIn Template

**Connection request note:**

> Hi [First Name], I help [niche] businesses in [City] upgrade their web presence and get more leads. Would love to connect.

**Follow-up message (after they accept):**

> Thanks for connecting, [First Name]. Quick question—are you happy with how your current site is performing, or is it more of a digital brochure right now?

### In-Person Script (Chamber of Commerce, local meetup)

> “I specialize in building websites for [niche]. Most of my clients come to me because their current site isn’t bringing in business. I’d love to show you what a modern site looks like—do you have a card?”

**Action:** Send 30 emails and 20 LinkedIn messages today. Track them in a simple spreadsheet.

## Day 5: Run Your First Sales Calls

When someone bites, book a 15-minute call. Keep it simple.

### Sales Call Structure (The “15-Minute Close”)

1. **Build rapport** (2 min): Ask about their business, how long they’ve been around.
2. **Diagnose** (5 min): “What’s working with your current site? What’s not?” Listen more than you talk.
3. **Show the demo** (5 min): Screen-share your Day 2 demo site. Explain how it solves their pain points.
4. **Quote and close** (3 min): Present the middle tier first. Ask: “Does the Business Site package sound like the right fit?”
5. **Handle objections**: Common ones:
   - *“I need to think about it.”* → “Totally fair. What specifically are you weighing?”
   - *“It’s too expensive.”* → “I get it. If I could show that one new customer covers the cost, would it change the math?”
   - *“My nephew builds websites.”* → “That’s great for a hobby. When your business depends on it, you want a pro.”

**Action:** Book 2–3 calls for Day 6.

---

## Day 6: Close and Collect

On the call, get a verbal yes, then send a simple invoice or payment link immediately.

**Invoice terms:**
- For monthly plans: first month + setup fee upfront.
- For one-time builds: 50% deposit to start, 50% on completion.

**Pro tip:** Use Stripe, PayPal, or your platform’s built-in billing. The faster you collect, the fewer deals evaporate.

**Action:** Close your first client. Send contract (use a free template from Bonsai or AND CO).

---

## Day 7: Build and Deliver

Now the real work begins. But with a white-label platform, “real work” is usually 4–8 hours.

1. **Gather assets** (logo, photos, copy from client).
2. **Build the site** using your chosen template.
3. **Optimize**: Set page titles, meta descriptions, and alt tags.
4. **Review**: Send a preview link to the client.
5. **Revise**: Limit to 1 round of revisions (state this upfront).
6. **Go live**: Point their domain. Collect final payment.
7. **Hand off**: Send login credentials and a short “how to edit” video (Loom is free).

**Time budget:** One focused day or two half-days.

---

## What to Do After Week 1

Your first client is proof you can do this. Now systematize:

- **Document your process.** Turn Day 7 into a checklist.
- **Ask for referrals.** “Do you know any other [niche] owners who need a site?”
- **Add testimonials.** A 2-sentence quote from a happy client is worth more than a fancy portfolio.
- **Raise your prices.** After 3 clients, your experience justifies a higher tier.
- **Consider switching platforms.** If you outgrow SiteSwan’s margins or white-label limits, platforms like Nida are built for agencies that want to scale without per-site fees.
